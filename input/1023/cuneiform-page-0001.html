BENSONOMYS, CALOMYS, AND THE ORIGIN OF THE PHYLLOTINE GROUP OF NEOTROPICAL CRICETINES (RODENTIA: CRICETIDAE)

JON A. BASKIN

AssTItAcT.â€” The Late Miocene to Pleistocene North American genus Bensonornys is recognized as a subgenus of the Pleistocene and Recent South American genus Calornys. Two new species of Calornys are described from the Hemphillian White Cone fauna of Arizona. Characters of this older material indicate that Calomys is derived from Copemys, the cricetine which is also ancestral to Perornyscus. The phyllo- tine group of South American cricetines initially evolved in North America and did not enter South America until the Panamanian land bridge was established, in the latest Pliocene or earliest Pleistocene. Ehgmodontia arizonae Gidley (1922), from early Blancan deposits of the St. David Formation near Benson, Arizona, was made the type species of the genus Bensonomys (Gazin, 1942). Gazin nonetheless noted the many resemblances between the extinct North American genus Bensonomys and the Recent South American phyllotine Eligmodontia. Since that time these similarities have been ascribed to parallelism (Hershkovitz, 1966a; Hibbard, 1972). Characters of older material collected from the Bidahochi Formation at White Cone Peak, Arizona, indicate a close relationship as originally supposed. Bensonomys is herein considered congeneric with Caiomys, an- other South American phyllotine. The two new species of Calomys from White Cone described in this paper are the earliest known members of the Hesperomyini, the dominantly South American tribe of the Cricetinae.

GEOLOGIC SETTING

White Cone Peak is situated on the joint-use land of the Navajo and Hopi Indian tribes in Navajo County, northeastern Arizona. The White Cone fauna was collected from the upper member of the Bidahochi Formation, approximately 20 m above the volcanic (limburgite tuff) middle member (Repenning et al., 1958). The fauna is characterized by the beaver Dipoides williamsi Stirton (1936) and a mylagaulid (Lance, 1954) and is assigned to the Hemphillian Land Mammal Age. Evernden et al. (1964) reported a date of 4.1 million years for the middle member of the Bidahochi Formation. The basalt that was used to determine this date, however, was not from the middle member, but came from a diatreme within the upper member of the Bidahochi Formation. The basalt is unconformable on the upper member where remnants of this diatreme are exposed. A basalt which can be traced into the middle member of the Bidahochi Formation at Roberts Mesa, approximately 7 mi northwest of White Cone, was dated by R. Scarborough of the Department of Geosciences of the University of Arizona at 6.69 ~ 0.20 million years (Scarborough et al., 1974). This date places White Cone in the middle Hemphillian. It is no longer the youngest radiometrically dated Hemphillian fauna.
