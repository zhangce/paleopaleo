Hershkovitz rejected a North American origin for the Hesperomyini because none of the living Peromyscini have a complex-type penis. However, possession of a complex- type penis is presumed to have been the primitive condition in the Cricetidae (Hooper and Musser, 1964). The simple-type penis can be derived from the complex-type by loss of the lateral digits, and this has occurred independently in many different genera and species of Cricetidae (Hershkovitz, 1966b). Democricetodon of the Late Miocene of Europe is synonomous with Copemys of the Late Miocene of North America (Fahlbusch, 1967). The baculum and penis type of Copemys are as yet unknown but they are presumed to have been complex because the Old World descendants of Copemys (=Democricetodon), the Cricetini (Mein and Freudenthal, 1971), have a complex-type penis. In the New World, simplification occurred in the peromyscine radiation from Copemys. The complex penis was retained in the hesperomyine radia- tion. Diversification of the Hesperomyini had begun in North America at least seven million years ago, long before the completion of the Panamanian land bridge. Ben- sonomys represents a part of that radiation that was taking place in the southern portion of North America much as Patterson and Pascual suggested. Further peripheral records of this evolution are the Early Blancan appearance of a sigmodont, Sigmodon medius, and a possible akodont, Symmetrodontomys simplicidens, of the Rexroad fauna of Kansas before they are known from South America. With the completion of the Panamanian land bridge, members of this initial radiation entered South America where they further diversified in many new habitats.

There is no reason to hypothesize that this hesperomyine radiation began prior to about 10 million years ago. The hesperomyine tooth pattern cannot be directly derived from any cricetid rodent known prior to the Late Clarendonian. Copemys and the aberrant and poorly known Gnomomys are the only two genera of cricetines known from the Barstovian and Clarendonian of North America (Lindsay, 1972). During the Clarendonian, the dental pattern of Copemys began to simplify, losing accessory lophs(-ids) and styles(-ids) (Lindsay, 1972). During the Hemphillian, alignment of the protoconid-entoconid and paracone-hypocone was achieved to form the peromyscine (Jacobs, 1977) or hesperomyine type tooth.

Black (1963) reported the possible presence of much earlier (Hemingfordian) hes- peromyine cricetines from the Thomas Farm fauna of Florida. He noted the re- semblances of an upper molar fragment to Sigmodon and of an Ml to a peromys- cine or hesperomyine. These teeth are from Pleistocene deposits containing Platygonus which also occur at Thomas Farm. The M, belongs to Peromyscus flori- danus. The upper molar fragment belongs to a small, relatively high crowned Sigmodon, perhaps S. bakeri. Other lophate supposedly cricetine teeth of the Oligocene and Miocene have proven to be deciduous teeth of geomyoids (Black, 1969). The tendency towards simple lophate teeth in cricetines did not begin until the Late Clarendonian or Hemphillian.

Patterson and Pascual (1972) supported and elaborated Simpson's arguments, pos- tulating a tropical North American center of cricetine diversification in the Clarendo- nian and Hemphillian, with Chapadmalalan immigration of an already diversified cricetine fauna into South America. Hershkovitz (1966a, 1969, 1972) misinterpreted this and attributed to them just a single Central American species emigrating to South America. Although vertebrate paleontologists have been unable to cite positive proof for their hypothesis, the absence of such evidence does not indicate that this theory is wrong. Late Tertiary fossiliferous terrestrial deposits in tropical North America are very limited. To date they have produced no small mammals (Ferrusquia-Villafranca, 1975), but the large mammals wholly corroborated Simpson's history of faunal inter- change. White Cone is one of the southernmost known North American Hemphillian rodent faunas and provides the first peripheral records of what may be a Central American Hemphillian cricetine radiation (see also Jacobs, 1977). The close relation- ship of Hemphillian and Blancan species of Calomys (Bensonomys) in North America to Chapadmalalan and younger species of Calomys (Calomys) in South America pro- vides positive evidence in support of Simpson's as well as Patterson and Pascual's view.

The presence of supernumerary roots on the White Cone Calomys (Bensonomys) removes Hibbard's (1972) objection to Bensonomys having a close relationship with neotropical cricetines. Peromyscine teeth typically have three upper and two lower roots. Hesperomyini can have four upper and three or four lower roots on their cheek teeth. Supernumerary roots are not known in Copemys.
