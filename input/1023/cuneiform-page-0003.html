A deeper lower jaw is characteristic of more primitive species of genera derived from Copemys (see below) such as Peromyscus valensis (Shotwell, 1967). The only characteristic of this diagnosis separating Bensonomys from Calomys is lower incisor more procumbent, which is not sufficient for generic distinction.

Hibbard (1972:92) diagnosed Bensonomys as follows: "About the size of living Eligmodontia typus Cuvier 1837, of South America, which has a four-rooted M,. Ben- sonomys has only two roots on the M,. Tooth pattern is more similar to Eligmodontia than Peromyscus, a case of parallel development." This distinction based on root number is not sufficient, as supernumerary roots are sometimes present on the two new species of Calomys (Bensonomys) from White Cone and on C. (B.) arizonae. The dental pattern of fossil Bensonomys is generically indistinguishable from recent Calomys. Bensonomys is retained as a primitive North American subgenus of Calomys in which the consistent presence of supernumerary roots had not been established. Its older temporal position is in accord with its morphology.

Calomys (Bensonomys) is distinguished from Peromyscus in having a well- developed bilobed anterocone(-id) on Mi anterior position of the masseteric crest, and mental foramen very high on the dentary. It is distinguished from Baiomys in having relatively wider molars and a well-developed bifurcated anteroconid on M,.

Calomys (Bensonomys) is distinguished from Copemys in having alignment or near alignment of protolophule II with the anterior arm of the hypocone, and alignment of entolophid with the posterior arm of the protoconid and elongation and bilobation of the anteroconid of M,. Copemys is ancestral to Peromyscus (Clark et al., 1964; Lindsay, 1972), Onychomys (Hoffmeister, 1959), and other genera of peromyscines.

Calomys (Bensonomys) has a pronounced reduction in accessory lophs(-ids) and styles(-ids) in comparison to Copemys. Mesolophs and mesostyles occur only in two Hemphillian species, C. yazhi and C. gidleyi. These two also have a very slight nonalignment between protolophule II and the anterior arm of the hypocone. These characters support a Copemys ancestry for Bensonomys. Bensonomys was probably derived from the peromyscine group characterized by small size and a slightly asym- metrical anteroconid that includes Copemys pagei, C. dentalis, and Peromyscus valensis. Bensonomys differs from these in having a more elongate, consistently bilobed anterocone and anteroconid.

Eligmodontia arizonae Gidley (1922) from the Blancan Benson fauna of Arizona was made the type species of the genus Bensonomys (Gazin, 1942). Hibbard (1956) included two additional species, B. eliasi and B. meadensis, from Blancan de- posits of Kansas. Calomys (B.) meadensis also occurs in Blancan deposits in Nebraska (Hibbard, 1972). Cimarronomys stirtoni Hibbard (1953) from the earliest Blancan Saw Rock Canyon Fauna of the Rexroad Formation of Kansas is refer- able to Calomys (Bensonomys). Differences between it and C. arizonae are less than those between C. yazhi and C. arizonae. Cimarronomys is therefore synonymous with Calomys. Characters which differentiate C. stirtoni from other species of C. (Ben- sonomys) are broader, deeper valley between anteroconid and metaconid (Hibbard, 1953) plus larger size. Characters of Ms and M8 are similar to those of C. yazhi and C. bradyi .

Hibbard also apparently intended to refer POryzomys pliocaenicus from the late Hemphillian Edson fauna of Kansas to Bensonomys (Hershkovitz 1966a:737), but I have been unable to find a formal redesignation. The holotype (MCZ 6202) is a mandi- ble with teeth greatly worn and only the outline of cusps remaining. M;M8 length is approximately the same as that of the holotype of Calomys meadensis. The teeth are all two rooted. The Edson fauna is currently being restudied.
