ORIGIN OF THE NEOTROPICAL HESPEROMYINI

New World Cricetidae of the subfamily Cricetinae are often placed in three tribes (Patterson and Pascual, 1972) â€” Neotomini, Peromyscini, and Hesperomyini. The Neotomini have a complex Ms . They are probably Hemphillian Eurasian immigrants (Jacobs, 1977). The Peromyscini are the North American group of simple-penis type cricetines. Copemys, the earliest peromyscine, emigrated from Eurasia to North America during the Barstovian (Fahlbusch, 1967; Lindsay, 1972). The Hesperomyini are the dominantly neotropical group of complex-penis type cricetines and are known from Hemphillian to Recent in North America and from Chapadmalalan (Late Blancan and Irvingtonian) to Recent in South America.

The origin of the Hesperomyini has been the subject of much debate. Hershkovitz (1966a, 1969) postulated that the ancestors of the hesperomyines entered South America from North America in the Early Miocene or earlier and diversified in situ, with subsequent migration back into Central and North America beginning in the Blancan. Hershkovitz believed an early colonization was required to explain the high diversity and specialization of the approximately 40 genera and 180 species of living South American cricetines.

In his most recent paper on the origin of the neotropical hesperomyines, Hersh- kovitz (1972) proposed an early Tertiary colonization of South America by African cricetids. This is virtually impossible. Undoubted cricetid rodents are not known from anywhere before the Oligocene and do not appear in Africa until the Miocene (Lavocat, 1973). Hershkovitz favored an African ancestry for the neotropical Hesperomyini because none of the living Peromyscini have a complex-type penis and because the dentition of Mystromys, a Pliocene to Recent African cricetid, has a broad resemblance to that of some phyllotines. This comparison, even if correct, would imply a relatively recent (Blancan or younger) interchange with Africa. However, Hershkovitz overlooked the fact that Mystromys has a simplified penis with a undigitate baculum (Hooper and Musser, 1964; Vorontsov, 1966), an entepicondylar foramen on the humerus (Vor- ontsov, 1966), and the mental foramen low on the dentary. All of these features indicate that Mystromys is at least as distantly related to the phyllotines as is Peromyscus. Lavocat (1973) included Mystromys within his African section of the Cricetodontinae because of similarities of the skulls of the Miocene genus Afrocricetodon and Mys- tromys. The dentition of Mystromys is derived from African Miocene cricetodontids and is similar to that of phyllotines because of convergent adaptation to semi-arid grassland habitats. Hershkovitz's hypotheses are tenable only if the fossil record is ignored.

The fossil record, as presented by Simpson (1950), shows that South America was an isolated island continent during most of the Tertiary. The only Tertiary mammals known to have reached South America by waif dispersal before the completion of the Panamanian land bridge were the caviomorph rodents and the ceboid monkeys in the Oligocene and procyonids in the Late Miocene.
