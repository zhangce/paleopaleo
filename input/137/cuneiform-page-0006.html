Terms have in the past been applied to the cephalic border morphology of brachymetopids following two separate inter- pretations of homology. Owens and Thomas (1975, text-figure 2) argued that the broad cephalic doublure of brachymetopids represents growth inwards underneath and beyond the true ce- phalic border furrow. Following this view, the deep outer furrow of species like Cordania falcara Whittington, 1960 (e.g., Camp- bell, 1977, plate 5, figures 2b, 3b) is considered the true border furrow, while a shallower inner furrow marking the inner extent of the doublure is considered a secondarily acquired paradoub- lural line.

In contrast to this opinion, Campbell (1977) considered the furrow aligned with the inner edge of the doublure to be the true border furrow.
