Brachiopod faunas have been recognized as the "Clarkeia Fauna" by many workers (Cocks, 1972; Berry and Boucot, 1972; Cocks and McKerrow, 1973; Boucot, 1975). Most of them indicated the probable cli- matic origin of this endemism, considering the fauna a cold water one (Berry and Boucot, 1972; Cocks and Fortey, 1990).

New taxa recovered from both the Silurian Precordillera and northwestern Argentina basins prove that faunal diversity is higher than previously considered (Sanchez et al., 1991). How- ever, brachiopod diversity does not reach the values of coeval Faunas from the North Silurian Realm (i.e., Watkins, 1979). Similar low-diversity values characterize the bivalve fauna, in which many taxa such as the Cardiolidae (a common component of Bohemian Silurian faunas; Kriz, 1979) are lacking or poorly represented (i.e., Pteriacea, Antipleuridae). Comparison with Silurian bivalves from Bolivia shows that those faunas have a similar low diversity. Higher latitudes account for the differences in diversity and abundance between Malvinokaffric (cold water) and North Si- lurian Realm temperate water bivalve fauna. The occurrence of taxa such as Dualina in Bolivia and Argentina (Pojeta et al., 1976; Sanchez, 1991b) could be due to surface currents carrying eurythermal species from temperate to cold waters. Differences between Argentine-Bolivian (southern) and Venezuela (north- ern extreme of South America) faunas can be interpreted in the same manner (palaeoclimatic gradient), when following the con- tinental arrangement of Smith, Briden, and Drewry (1973) or Van der Voo (1988), with the northern edge of South America lying at lower paleolatitudes than the southern extremity. In more recent paleogeographic reconstructions (Scotese and McKerrow, 1990), rotation of the landmasses places Venezuela at higher paleolatitudes than the previously mentioned arrange- ments; consequently, latitudinal differences between the north- ern and southern regions of South America are reduced.
