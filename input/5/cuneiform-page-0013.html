PALEOECOLOGY

During life, a thin leathery integument probably enclosed the calcite skeleton. The thinness and the transparency of the skin tains abundant remains of Pentremites Say and crinoids, bra- chiopods, and horn corals. The clay rests directly on a 7 m thick limestone of the middle Chesterian Glen Dean Formation. The clay changes laterally into a well laminated dark gray shale which contains abundant specimens of Pentremites pyriformis Say but lacks Pentremites robustus Lyon.

The specimens of Pentremites robustus Lyon are large, up to 4 cm. About 15 of 75 specimens are undeformed. The remainder are slightly to greatly crushed. Of these 75 calyces, ten show good to excellent color markings. Several of the Pentremites pyriformis specimens also show color markings but all have type 2 markings. The type 2 color pattern is not present on any spec- imens of Pentremites robustus Lyon. The abundance of color markings on specimens from the clay is attributed to its cohe- siveness and relative impermeability.

A comprehensive literature search for color markings on ca- lyces of modern echinoderms similar to those described herein has been unsuccessful except for the references cited. It is well known that modern crinoids are brightly colored and blastoids likely were also, although proof is lacking. The difficulty of comparing the coloring of modern crinoids to blastoids is be- cause the modern crinoid calyx is small, and the brachials, arms and pinnules compose the bulk of the animal; thus, illustrations of color markings on the calyx are not common (Meyer and Macurda, 1980).
