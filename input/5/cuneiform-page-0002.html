However, raised areas on the specimens may well have been observable through the integument. The color patterns are considered to be original because many specimens have identical markings at the same positions. The markings do not appear to be related to any particular lithology or stratigraphic setting, al- though most of them were collected from shallow marine shales or clays.

COLOR PATTERNS

Type 1 color pattern, representing the most common type in the collection of specimens (51), is exhibited by specimens of Penrremires godoni (Defrance) from the lower Chesterian Ri- denhower and Elwren Formations of Illinois and Kentucky, and by Pentremites tulipaformis Hambach from the middle Chester- ian Glen Dean Formation of Kentucky. This pattern is charac- terized by horizontal bands of darker calcite occurring on the main body of the individual radials (Figure 1.1 â€” 1.6). These darker bands cross from one radial to the adjoining radial and terminate near the ambulacra. These horizontal bands may num- ber 10 â€” 15 or more in vertical succession. Close examination of the bands suggest that the darker bands may be slightly raised in some specimens but this is very indistinct.

On the deltoids, the darker bands are displayed as arched (convex) chevrons (Figure 1.1 â€” 1.6). The chevrons may be sharp- ly arched or more gently curved. In our specimens, the vertical succession of arches ranges from several to eight or more.

The pattern on the basals consists of darker bands radiating aborally from the aboral tip of each ambulacrum toward the stem facet (Figure 1.7, 1.8). Like the horizontal bands on the radials, these bands may be smooth or indistinctly raised. Because of the radiating bands beneath each aboral portion of each ambu- lacrum, the result is a triangular pattern beneath each interradial region (Figure 1.3, 1.7, 1.8).
