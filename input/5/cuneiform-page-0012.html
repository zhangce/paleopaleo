Color markings have been described in other fossil inverte- brates that are apparently similar to those on our specimens. Color patterns on mollusca are the most common, but they have also been observed on many other groups including trilobites and echinoderms such as starfish, echinoids, and crinoids. In 1893, Bather mentioned the occurrence of dark spots on the arms of the crinoid Cyathocrinus Miller. Foerste (1930) described and illustrated color markings of fossil cephalopods and brachio- pods, with notes on gastropods, pelecypods, and a single trilobite specimen. His description states that in Paleozoic fossils, the former presence of colors "is usually indicated only by varying shades of light and dark." Sometimes, these traces of color are in the form of dots or blotches, irregularly scattered, or arranged according to some definite pattern. At other times, they may take the form of radiating or concentric lines or bands, or of com- binations of the two. Occasionally, the lines are of a zigzag form, or the patterns may be even more intricate. This description is similar to what the authors have observed in Mississippian Ches- tcrian Pentremites Say.

They provide an excellent sum- mary of the organic pigments in invertebrates, stating that "color in modern organisms is produced by a large number of organic pigments." In fossils, pigments found in "hard parts are the most likely to be preserved, and then only if the organic chem- istry of the pigment and the subsequent diagenetic history of both the pigment and the hard parts are favorable." Melanin is a pigment widely present in invertebrates in both hard and soft tissue. Also, tetrapyroles occur as biochromes in hard tissues. Starfish contain porphyrins in their integuments.
