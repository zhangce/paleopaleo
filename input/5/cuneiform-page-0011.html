The color patterns on the genera described by Reimann (1961) and Sprinkle (1973) are similar in that they consist of "surface color markings form- ing bands of tiny growth lines." The color pattern on Pentrem- ites conoideus Hall, illustrated by Sprinkle (1973, text-fig. 19), from the middle Mississippian, appears to be identical to that illustrated in our type 2 (Figure 2.4 â€” 2.6), a specimen of Pen tremites godoni (Defrance), from the lower Chesterian Elwren Formation of Hardin Springs, Kentucky.

Sprinkle (1973) notes that the light and dark bands conform to thc growth lines and interprets that the alternating light and dark bands represent seasonal (yearly) growth, suggesting that the individual might be three to seven years in age. This may be correct for type 2 blastoid patterns; however, similar specu- lation cannot be applied to type 1 patterns since they have (Fig- ure 2.4 â€” 2.6) arched or convex alternating light and dark bands which do not conform to the growth lines but are in direct op- position to them. In the same specimens, the pattern on the ra- dials consists of horizonal alternating light and dark bands which also do not conform to the growth lines, but rather directly cross them. In addition the type 4 specimen of Pentremites robustus Lyon (Figure 2.15, 2.16) has several rows of dark dots concen- tric to each ambulacrum which do conform to the growth lines.

In summary, some patterns of alternating light and dark bands do conform to the growth lines, but others do not. This suggests that some and perhaps all the color patterns do not represent seasonal (yearly) growth.

The deeply etched grabenlike depressions in the plate surface of Meristoschisma Sprinkle may be related to those sometimes observed in Pentremites robustus Lyon (type 3). Sprinkle (1973) postulates that these etched depressions in the plate surface of Meristoschisma Sprinkle may be a periodic interruption in the primary growth of all the plates of the echinoderm and that structure or composition of the calcite secreted during growth of the plate changes periodically, possibly representing a change in pore size of the echinoderm microstructure or a change in chemical composition of the secreted calcite (perhaps magnesi- um content). This may be correct. It is notable that the etching in Pentremites robustus Lyon (Figure 2.9) conforms exactly to the dark area of the specimen lateral to the aboral end of the ambulacra.
