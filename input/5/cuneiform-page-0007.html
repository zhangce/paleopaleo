NATURE OF COLOR PATTERNS

To determine the nature of the color markings in Pentremites Say, a specimen of Pentremites tulipaformis Hambach with well displayed color markings was cut transversely from sumjmit to base, so as to traverse as many dark bands as possible, approx- imately 12 dark bands (Figure 3.11). In this transverse section, the dark markings or bands appear to represent a thin layer of darker calcite on the exterior of the calyx. Several applications of dilute hydrochloric acid to the color markings on this speci- men partially removed the color patterns. Thus, the coloring does not deeply penetrate the skeleton of the specimens. How- ever, the color patterns may be well preserved on the ambulacra where the ambulacral covering plates are not present (Figure 2.3 â€” 2.6). These patterns on the ambulacra have been faintly seen on Pentremites robustus Lyon from the upper Chesterian lower Tar Springs Formation, 4.8 km north of Eckerty, Indiana, and are even more pronounced on Pentremites godoni (Defrance) from the lower Chesterian Elwren Formation at Hardin Springs, Kentucky. This is not just random coloration because the pat- terns on adjacent ambulacra are the same (Figure 3.4 â€” 3.6).
