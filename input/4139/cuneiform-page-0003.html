Sedimentological interpretations of the Garnett site were made by
Peabody (1952, 1957) in connection with his excavations of the vertebrates
and by Reisz et al. (1982). The site was first interpreted as a lagoon into
which the vertebrates had been rafted by a slowly moving river {Peabody,
1952). This interpretation has recently been modified by Reisz et al. {1982).
According to the new interpretation (Reisz et al., 1982, p.749), the lower
part of the deposit was laid down "... in a shallow forested stream valley or
channel which was eroded into a limestone coastal plain during the later
stages of a ma>or eustatic fall in sea level." When sea level rose, the valley
became an estuary and the conifer forest was replaced by estuarine mudflats
(Reisz et al., 1982). The plant fossils which were collected came from the
mud flat facies.

MATERIALS AND METHODS

Specimens were collected in 1980 and 1981 at the "Garnett site" six miles (10 km) northwest of Garnett, Kansas on the Hardesty farm (Garnett West quadrangle, T20S, R19D, Sec. 5, NE, NW, NW) from the mud flat facies (Reisz et al., 1982) of the Rock Lake Shale Member of the Stanton Lime- stone Formation, Lansing Group, Missourian Stage, Upper Pennsylvanian. The site is a small quarry made for the collection of vertebrate fossils. The macrofossils referred to in this study will be deposited in the Invertebrate Paleontology Collection of the Royal Ontario Museum, Toronto, with numbers ROM42331 to ROM42930.

Macro fossiLs

Plant megafossils were collected from a 1-m' area through a thickness of
1.45 m for quantitative study. The area was chosen because of the relatively
large thickness of the Rock Lake Shale Member and because of its proximity
to the former position of a large column of rock which had been removed
for sedimentological and palynological study. Positions of all identifiable (at
the generic level) plant fossils were recorded both with respect to depth,
lateral positions, and orientation. Size was not a criterion. Rock layers were
split at 0.5 â€” 1.0 cm intervals except in certain limestone layers which could
not be split more finely than 2.0 cm. Isolated conifer leaves were treated as
unidentifiable because of their great abundance although cuticular studies
might have allowed a generic identification in some cases. Because of its
rarity, all fern-like fohage was collected even if it could not be identified.
Large fossils were collected in thick blocks; hence the layers immediately
beneath these fossils have not been sampled. This has been considered in the
analysis.

Only 94% of the fossils were actually collected. The others broke into
unidentifiable fragments during attempts to conect them. The identities and
locations of the uncollected fossils were recorded. All of these fossils were
quite small. In addition, one specimen was destroyed during shipment to the

Micro fossils

A 1.49-m thick column of the Rock Lake Shale Member was collected by Malcolm J. Heaton of the Alberta Palaeontological Museum and Research Institute, Drumheller, Alberta, Canada, for sedimentological and palyno- logical study. Palynological preparations were made (on average) for each 4 cm interval by the University of Toronto Palynological Laboratory and provided for this study. Counts for the miospores at each level included more than 200 specimens except where miospores were too rare or absent (above a depth of 92 cm). The locations of all miospores on slides correspond to the coordinates on a Leitz Ortholux microscope. Shde preparations used in the counts are located in the Paleobotany Laboratory (Morrill Hall), Umversity of Illinois, Urbana, under the numbers UIUC19872 to UIUC20111.

The fossils in the top 20 cm were destroyed by weathering. The absence of miospores in the top 90 cm of the deposit is probably due to poor extrac- tion techniques. Dr. Russel Peppers of the Illinois State Geological Survey has been able to extract miospores from samples with which the University of Toronto palynological laboratory was unsuccessful. Due to lack of time, no data were collected from the slide prepared by Dr. Peppers.
