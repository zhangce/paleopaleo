The coal swamp floras of North America showed significant changes at the
transition from the Middle to Late Pennsylvanian. The Early and Middle
Pennsylvanian coal swamps were dominated by the arborescent lycopods
Leptdophloios and Lepidodendron while the Late Pennsylvanian ones were
dominated by the marattiaceous tree ferns Psaronius {Phillips et al., 1974).

The extinction of I epidophloios and Lepitjodendron may have been caused by the onset of a drier chmate in the Middle â€” Late Pennsylvanian transition. Lepidodendron and I epidophlotos were apparently well adapted to reproduction in wet environments with then waterAispersed megasporo- phyll units (Phillips, 1979). The fust part of the Late Pennsylvanian {Steph- aman of Western Europe) was apparently considerably drier than the Middle Pennsylvanian (Westphahan). This is consistent with the stratigraphic distri- bution of identified bituminous coal resources (Phillips et al., 1980). These coal resource patterns are observed in the Western Interior, the Eastern Interior, and in the Appalachian Coal Regions of the United States. Reduc- tion in coal deposits occurs first in the west and progressively later towards the east. The Western Interior Coal Region was probably the driest of the three regions so that it was fust affected by the drying trend {Philhps et al., 1980).

A similar paleoclimatic pattern was suggested by Read and Mamay (1964) based on the study of compression floras. Because of the rarity of lycopods in the Pennsylvanian of the western United States and the occurrence of conifers after the beginning of orogeny m the Late Pennsylvanian, they suggested that the climate of the Rocky Mountains region was drier than farther east in the mid-continent and the Appalachians.

The early Late Pennsylvanian dry interval ended near the onset of the Monongahelan time in the Appalachian Region. Significant amounts of coal did not form in the Western Interior Coal Region until shghtly later (Elmo and Nodaway Coal members).
