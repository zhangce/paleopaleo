EFFECT OF THE DRY INTERVAL ON LATE PENNSYLVANIAN COAL-SWAMP
FLORAS

The same climatic conditions which affected the upland vegetation had a profound influence on the coal swamps. The survival of the tree ferns through the dry interval and then rise to dominance in the Late Pennsylvanian coal swamps may be due to their reproductive biology. During the dry interval, coal swamps undoubtedly still existed but they were much smaller in size and more ephemeral than those of the wet intervals. Under such cucum- stances, colonizing ability was of great importance. The tree ferns obviously must have had an advantage over Lepidodendron and Lepidophloios in this respect. The tree ferns reproduced by spores which were probably wmd- dispersed while the megasporophyll units of Lepidodendron and Lepido- phloios were probably water-dispersed (Phillips, 1979). The much smaller size and wind dispersal of the tree fern spores enhanced their ability to colonize the small and short-lived coal swamps of the dry interval and so allowed them to survive when Lepujodendron and Lepidophloios became extinct. When widespread coal-swamp conditions returned in the Late Pennsylvanian, they were able to dominate the coal swamps because they no longer had to compete with Lepidodendron and Lepidophloios, The superior colonizing ability of the tree ferns, rather than their competitive ability, may have allowed them to survive through the dry interval and so allowed them to dominate the Late Pennsylvanian coal swamps.

During the dry mterval, certain plants which previously had not grown near depositional environments began to grow in places where they could be preserved. The conifers, at least, had been in existence earlier although they were very rare (Scott, 1974). The Garnett flora is one of the first occurrences of well-preserved floras containing plants which became more common in the Permian.
