The second and more extensive occurrence of upland floras occurred during the early Late Pennsylvanian. The Garnett flora is one of at least four such "upland" floras from Kansas from the dry interval (Cridland and Morris, 1963; Leisman, 1971; Remy et al., 1980). The kinds of plants appearing m the Garnett flora are mdicative of poorly known vegetative patterns which probably characterized extensive drier habitats during Pennsylvanian time but which were only preserved under exceptional cucumstances. The more frequent occurrence of conifer-rich floras from the late Missourian onwards, especially in the Permian, generally indicates a progressively drier envuon- ment on land in North America and Europe.

PREVIOUS STUDIES OF THE GARNETT AREA

This site has been previously studied with respect to its vascular plant fossils, palynology, vertebrate paleontology and invertebrate paleontology. Investigations of many aspects of the site, particularly the vertebrate fauna, are in progress under the direction of Robert R. Reisz of the University of Toronto.

The Garnett flora was first studied by Elias who listed a large number of plants from the Garnett site (Moore et al., 1936) including nineteen plant taxa which were considered to be new species or varieties. Some species were named and illustrated but none are described; all binomials are invalid except Dichophyllum moorei for which Elias provided a diagnosis (Moore et al., 1936, p.16). Additional systematic work on the Garnett flora has been per- formed by Florin (1939b, c, p.218), Andrews {1941), Baxter and Hartman (1954) and Remy et al. (1980).

The flora is dominated by the primitive conifer Lebachia garnettensis Cordaites is also quite common. Another common plant is Callipteris flabel- lifera ssp. mooret (= Dichophyllum moorei), which is probably a pterido- sperm since it has a dichotomy in its rachis. However, its highly dissected pmnules with long segments are unlike the foliage of the pteridosperms commonly found in roof shale floras of coal seams. Among the ca. 700 specimens collected, there were two specimens of lower vascular plants, Annularia asteris and Pecopteris sp.

Forty-seven miospore (= spores, pollen, and similar ob]ects with diameters of less than 200 pm) taxa have been reported from Rock Lake Shale {Gupta and Boozer, 1969). The most common miospore is the monosaccate pollen Potonieispontes, produced by Lebachia and other primitive conifers {Bharadwai, 1964a, b).

The Garnett site also contains a diverse assemblage of vertebrate fossils most of which are terrestnal. This assemblage includes at least four genera of pelycosaurs (Reisz et al., 1982), mMine invertebrates (Moore et al., 1936). at least four kinds of insects (Carpenter, 1933, 1940), and a scorpion {Moore et al., 1936; Elias, 1937). Additional details of the Garnett fauna are given by Moore (1964).

A sedimentological study of the Stanton Limestone Formation, of which the Rock Lake Shale is a member, has recently been completed (Heckel, 1975). The emphasis was placed on the limestone members of the formation, especially in Montgomery County, Kansas and in the adjacent portion of Oklahoma (approximately 80 km south of the Garnett site). The limestone members were interpreted as phylloid, algal mounds, while the shale deposits were due to terrigenous influx. Rock Lake Shale deposition was closed by a regression of the sea. A subsequent transgression initiated the deposition of the overlying South Bend deposits (Heckel, 1975).
