Taeniopteris, like the previously mentioned genera, appears to be rare in roof shale floras of coal seams. It is sometimes, but not invariably, associated with conifers (Cridland and Morris, 1963). It too probably grew in dner, i.e., mesic conditions.

The Cordaites species are a large and diverse group of plants ranging from upland floras to marine-influenced coal swamps (PhQhps, 1980). The abun- dance of Cordaites near the top of the section is consistent with a slightly moister local environment when the upper portion of the Rock Lake Shale was deposited.

Fern-like foliage was produced by ferns and ptendosperms. Such foliage is found m coal ball and roof shale floras of coal seams. The rarity of fern-like foliage throughout the deposit may mean that they generally could not live outside the moist environments.

The most distinct change in the microflora is between the samples in the upper and lower parts of the deposit. In the lower part of the deposit, striate pollen, such as the common Permian genus Protohaploxypinus, is a very important part of the microflora. In the upper part, it is largely replaced by non-striate, bisaccate pollen, mainly Vesicaspora and to a lesser extent, by Punctatisporites, a fern spore.

Vesicaspora is known to be produced by the Callistophytales (Millay and Taylor, 1974; Rothwell, 1981) which occur in coal balls, while foliage of the type which they produce also occurs in the roof shale floras of coal seams.
