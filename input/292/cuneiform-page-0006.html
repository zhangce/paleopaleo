DISCUSSION

A considerable number of species of the genus Aturia have been found in the Paleogene formations of the world except for the arctic regions. Concerning the Neogene Aturia species, Aturia aturi (Basterot, 1825) and Aturia cubaensis (Lea, 1841) had world-wide distribution during the early Miocene and the mid- dle Miocene (Jung, 1966; Beu, 1973). These species were on the decline in the middle Miocene (Sturani, 1958; Tomida, 1992). Most of the Neogene Aturia species became extinct at the mid- dle-upper Miocene boundary. The extinction of these species was probably caused by a marked regression and cooling of surface waters brought about by the intensi6ed Antarctic gla- ciation (Robin, 1988). Only two species of A. cubaensis (Lea, 1841) and A. coxi Miller, 1947, were able to survive to the end of the Miocene in the Pacific region (Beu, 1973). The last oc- currences of Aturia coxi Miller, 1947 are known from the up- permost Miocene of Australia and Japan. In Japan more than ten specimens, including adults and juveniles, have been ob- tained to date from the bathyal deposits yielding Acesta goliath (Sowerby, 1883) and Perotrochus sp. in the Senhata Formation of the Miura Group in the Boso Peninsula, central Japan (Tom- ida, 1983, 1989). The occurrence of a juvenile complete shell, 18 mm in diameter, suggests that Aturia coxi may have lived in the adjacent Sea of Japan prior to the deposition of the en- closing sediments.
