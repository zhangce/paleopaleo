Enhydra also has a thin, sharp ridge on the posterolateral edge of the shaft that begins one-third of the way down the shaft and ex- tends to the fibular facet. This ridge is present on the distal half of the shaft in the fossil, but it is much weaker. The most apparent difter- ence in the tibiae of these two taxa is the greatly elongated medial malleolus in the re- cent sea otter, compared to the relatively short, blunt process in the fossil. The smaller medial malleolus in the fossil is, however, partially a result of post-mortem abrasion. The tibiae of Enhydra and Enhydritherium are quite similar and support our suggestion that these taxa are closely related.

Pilgrim (1931) tentatively referred to En- hydriodon the distal epiphysis of a juvenile tibia from Pikermi, Greece although as with the Bone Valley specimen, there were no as- sociated teeth to confirm the generic assign- ment.
