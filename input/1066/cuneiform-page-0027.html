Most often, fossils occur as isolated finds on spoil piles or in "hydraulic wells," the latter being areas where the phosphatic matrix is turned into a slurry using jets of water under high pressure. The first four of these mines have produced the most diverse late Hemphillian mammal assemblages known from this re- gion.

In the past, the lack of strict stratigraphic control of fossils from the Bone Valley For- mation has not caused significant problems in biostratigraphic correlation since the ma- jorityy of terrestrial mammals known from this region compare closely with taxa from well known late Hemphillian faunas elsewhere in North America. Although no comprehensive study of the Upper Bone Valley mammals has been undertaken, several recent papers have considerably increased our knowledge of the composition and age of this fauna.
