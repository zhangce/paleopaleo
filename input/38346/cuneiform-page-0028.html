The Pliensbachian was the most intensely studied interval yielding four zones for the Early Pliensbachian and one zone for the Late Pliensbachian (Figs. 4 and 5); the Early Pliensbachian zones are the most precise and tightly constrained (Fig. 6). These four zones have been correlated to four assemblages/zones established for Early Pliensbachian ammonites. This implies that in closely sampled sequences with no evidence of tectonics, radiolarians may be equal to the precision of ammonites for biochronological dating.

The calibration for the Pliensbachian is based on ammonite-rich successions from the Queen Charlotte Islands (for ammonite data see Table 1 and Appendix B). The Early Toarcian zone is dated by superposition in North America (see 2.1.2.) and complemented by chemostratigraphy of the Mt. Mangart section in Slovenia (Sabatino et al., 2009; see 2.7.). The Middle-Late Toarcian zone is based on ammonite data in Carter et al. (1988). Toarcian radiolarian zones are herein correlated to Toarcian ammonite zones established by Jal<obs (1997) (Fig. 6). Aalenian zones are dated by ammonites in the Queen Charlotte Islands (Carter and Jal<obs, 'I 99'I ) and in the Betic Cordillera (Sandoval et al., 2008; see 2.5.).

Toarcian and Aalenian radiolarian zones are clearly broader than those established for the Pliensbachian. Factors contributing to this discrepancy include: (1) Toarcian and Aalenian faunas generally have been much less studied than Pliensbachian ones and fewer taxa of this age have been described; (2) fewer samples of Toarcian and Aalenian age were included in this study and amongst these, sampling intervals are generally much wider, and (3) because of regional tectonics in western North America, paleolatitudinal differences in the faunas are more apparent in the Toarcian-Aalenian and this has hindered strong global comparisons. For example, many new species were described from the Middle and Late Toarcian fauna of Queen Charlotte islands (Carter et al., 1988), but very few of these mid-latitude species have been found in low-latitude areas thereby limiting their use for global correlation.
