This lower fissile shale unit contains thin, allochthonous bio- clastic and oncolitic limestones, deposited in the quiet water en- vironment during storm events. Bioturbated silty mudstones and thin quartz sandstones become progressively more common in the middle and upper parts of this unit in the northern sections.

The middle unit, approximately 35 meters thick, is composed of oncolitic and silty limestone, and bioturbated silty mudstone. In the northern sections, two prominent limestone horizons occur within the bioturbated silty mudstones. The basal silty limestone was referred to as the "20-foot lime" in mining reports, or the "blue limestone marker" of Merriam (1964), while the upper, oncolitic limestone has previously been referred to in mining reports as the "10-foot lime." This oncolitic limestone and over- lying bioturbated silty mudstone and oolitic grainstones were referred to as the "oolitic zone" by Merriam (1964). In the Delamar Mountain sections, the basal silty limestone, or "blue limestone marker," is absent. Ledges of alternating silty and pure oncolitic limestone, capped by oolitic grainstones, dominate the upper half of the middle unit in the southern sections.

The upper unit begins at the base of a fissile shale interval overlying the oolitic zone of Merriam (1964). Fissile shale, bio- turbated silty mudstone with thin bioclastic and oncolitic lime- stone layers, and an upper quartz sandstone are the main con- stituents of this unit. The upper quartz sandstone is much thicker in the northern area. The top of the Grassy Spring Member is coincident with the top of the Pioche Shale and is drawn at the base of the overlying Lyndon Limestone, as defined by Merriam (1964).
