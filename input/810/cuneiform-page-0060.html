JERJNG is defined by Bottjer and Ausich (1986) as the ver- tical distribution of organisms within the benthic boundary layer. Secondary tierers are suspension-feeders positioned above or below the sediment-water interface, commonly as epizoans on primary-tier organisms (Bottjer and Ausich, 1986). Under- standing tiering in a suspension-feeding community helps to bet- ter define community structure and to identify the biological and ecological constraints relevant to an individual taxon. Secondary tiering on crinoids has been demonstrated and/or suggested by a number of workers (e.g., Grant, 1963, 1968; Rudwick, 1970; Halleck, 1973; Ausich and Bottjer, 1982; Meyer and Ausich, 1983; Bottjer and Ausich, 1986; Watkins, 1991). Sandy (1996) reported dense aggregations of atrypid brachiopods in a pre- sumed living relationship around an Upper Ordovician crinoid column. Brett and Eckert (1982) noted corals and crinoids at- tached to upright crinoid columns and reported Stegerhynchus attached to the base of living crinoids in the Rochester Shale (Wenlockian). Taylor and Brett (1996) described tiering and community succession in smothered-bottom assemblages from the Rochester Shale of western New York, and included mention of rhynchonellid and atrypid brachiopods, bryozoans, annelids, corals, platyceratid gastropods, and pelmatozoans as secondary tierers M.cGee and Watkins (1994) reported Eucalyptocrinites crassus columns, holdfasts, and crowns serving as secondary tiering surfaces for bryozoans, favositid corals, and commensal crinozoans in the Waldron Shale (Wenlockian).
