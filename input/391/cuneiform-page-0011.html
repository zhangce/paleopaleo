In his discussion of O. ramulosus, Springer (1920, p. 434) stated, "The three primibrachials feature is absolutely constant in this species throughout large numbers of specimens; as is also the four secundibrachials of the first interval, wherein it divers not only from the exscu/ptus group, but from the other species of this [ramulosus] group." We examined several specimens of O. ramulosus in the collections of the University of Cincinnati Geology Museum and found only a single specimen (Table I) in which one secundibrachial half-ray consists of three brachials. The remainder had four, in agreement with Springer s dictum. In O. grandis, all secundibrachial series consist of three bra- chials, except one with two. Springer's strong statement of con- stancy of four secundibrachials on O. ramulosus leads us to regard the presence of generally three secundibrachials in O. grandis as outside the limits of expected O. ramulosus variation.

A further reason for suspecting that the single specimen des- ignated as O. grandis represents a distinct species is that chron- ologic reduction in the number of primibrachials and secun- dibrachials has been demonstrated among Late Mississippian (Chesterian) species of Taxocrinus and Onychocri nus (Springer, 1920; Burdick and Strimple, 1971, 1973, 1982; Strimple, 1978; and Knox and Kendrick, 1987).
