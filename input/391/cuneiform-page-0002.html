subclasses of crinoids. Although they represent a relatively minor component of the crinoid fauna from the Middle Mis- sissippian Fort Payne Formation, 17 species are reported herein from south-central Kentucky and north-central Tennessee. Prior to this report, seven flexible crinoid species were known from the Fort Payne Formation from Whites Creek Spring, Davidson County, Tennessee, and Barren, Cumberland, and Hardin Counties, Kentucky.

New Fort Payne flexible crinoids are from nine localities dis- tributed among four facies types (Ausich and Meyer, 1990). Species diversity in each facies includes the following: five, cri- noid packstone buildups; three, wackestone buildups; three, fos- siliferous green shale facies; and two, sheetlike packstone facies. The report of these flexibles extends the geologic and geographic range of most taxa either from the New Providence Shale Mem- ber of the Borden Formation in north-central Kentucky and central Tennessee or from the Edwardsville Formation of the Borden Group in central and southern Indiana. Mespilocrinus kentuckyensis n. sp., Onychocrinus grandis n. sp., and Gaulo- crinus symmetros n. sp. are new occurrences.

Many of these crinoids are identified from a few incomplete specimens. Partial preservation of flexible individuals is typical, because calyx and arm plates in flexibles are only loosely su- tured. Meyer et al. (1989) demonstrated this taphonomic bias for flexibles, and this general bias is true for all flexibles discussed herein, except Gaulocrinus veryi and G. symmetros n. sp.
