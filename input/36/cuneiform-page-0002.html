It combines some of the material included in our previous paper on New Brunswick Heliolitida (Noble and Young, 1984) with extensive new collections from that region and from the south- ern Gaspe up to the base of the Late Silurian West Point For- mation, which was covered by Lee and Noble (1988). It is es- sential to redescribe and discuss the New Brunswick faunas here because of their strong similarities with Gaspe material of the same age, and because a much more intensive approach has considerably changed our interpretation of the family.

Along with this regional aspect, an important objective of this study is to consider these taxa in light of the present state of heliolitid systematics, and to assess their contribution to the resolution of systematic problems. The large number of new genera and upper-level taxa proposed in recent years needs to be carefully evaluated, and the analysis of the various aspects of morphological variation is an important part of such eval- uation. Here, variation is examined through qualitative and quantitative methods applied to large numbers of specimens and stratigraphic, paleoecologic, and geographic distributions are considered in some detail.

Most of the material described and illustrated was collected in the course of this study from measured sections in northern New Brunswick and the southern Gaspe Peninsula of Quebec (Figure 1). Other coralla collected over the past 15 years by K. D. M. Howells, H. J. Lee, and J. P. A. Noble are also included to enlarge the data base for analysis of variation. All but a few of the latter specimens could be well placed stratigraphically.

The Heliolitidae included in this study show few affinities with faunas from other parts of eastern North America and are most similar to those of northern Europe. Of the six species described, Heliolites laxus n. sp. and Heliolites sp. 1 are both restricted to the Chaleurs Bay region, while Heliolites distinctus n. sp. is found only on Anticosti Island. Heliolites interstinctus Linnaeus is widely distributed in northern Europe but is un- known elsewhere in eastern North America, and Heliolites sp. cf. H megastoma (M.'Coy) and Stelliporella sp. cf. S. lijlevtdli (Lindstrfim) are also probably conspecific with northern Euro- pean species.

Specimens collected in the course of this study are in the collection of the Geology Department, University of New Brunswick, Fredericton, New Brunswick. New type specimens are deposited in the National Type Fossil Collection at the Geo- logical Survey of Canada (GSC) in Ottawa. Other types exam- ined were borrowed from the GSC, the Yale Peabody Museum (YPM), and the Riksmuseum, Stockholm (RM).
