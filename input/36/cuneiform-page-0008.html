Heliolites interstinctus (Linnaeus) is used here to illustrate intraspecific variation because it is far more abundant than any of the other heliolitid species occurring in this region. The char- acters selected for bivariate scatter diagrams are ones commonly considered to have systematic significance, and include tabu- larium diameter, corallite spacing, tubule diameter, and wall thickness. Aspects of stratigraphic and geographic variation in this species are not dealt with here.

The bivariate plots (Figure 2) indicate that there is a general correlation in size of the diferent skeletal elements other than wall thickness. The area per corallite is closely tied to tabularium surface area (calculated from tabularium diameter for a more valid comparison), a correlation that becomes pronounced when more species are examined (see below). This correlation is not proportional (Figure 2.1), and Dixon (1974) noted a similar relationship in I'ropora from Anticosti Island. The rough linear relationship between tubule diameter and tabularium diameter in H. interstinctus (Figure 2.2) is also seen in H. distinctus.

Variation in wall thickness is dificult to deal with because it can be affected by preservation and because it varies considerably in cyclo- morphic colonies, and it is possible, if these factors could be discounted, that it would show some correlation with the other characters. There are no obvious differences between facies for any of the bivariate plots in Figure 2, which suggests that eco- typic variation was not a major component of the total variation.

Some understanding of interspecific numerical variation in heliolitids is also important because much comparison in the systematic literature has been based on numerical differences in such characters as tabularium diameter and corallite spacing. It is important to know if two species of a genus occurring in a single region show an overlap of numerical characters, so that specimens are not misidentified.
