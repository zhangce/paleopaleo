MORPHOLOGICAL VARIATION

Because the observed form of any organism is a product of a suite of factors, including genetic inheritance, age (ontogenetic stage), environment, basic structural (architectural) design, and chance (Raup, 1972), it is essential that variation is considered in any systematic study. It is difficult to establish the cause(s) of any particular observed variation, but some insight may be gained by the analysis of variation in individual morphological characters and by the comparison of taxa or characters. Most studies of heliolitid variation have been primarily interested in astogeny and ontogeny. Notable among the few that have con- sidered the systematic implications in any detail are the work of Dixon (1974) and Scrutton (in press).

The morphological simplicity of heliolitids and other Tabu- lata allows the use of quantified characters in the examination of variation, and variation is generally pronounced in these characters. Tabularium diameter, for example, often shows a doubling in its overall intraspeci6c range, and the ranges of colony means are also extensive. The degree of variation is not consistent in diferent species. For measured characters to be of value, it is essential that a large number of specimens are mea- sured so that the sample of variation is an adequate represen- tation of the population variation. Variation in characters that are not readily quantified is also of considerable importance, and is considered in the individual species discussions.
