PROCEDURES

All coralla were slabbed and polished, and acetate peels and a number of thin sections were made. Preservation is variable. The structure of many coralla is obscured by recrystallization and silicification but there are several well-preserved specimens of most species.

Terms used to describe internal morphology are in common usage and are derived from Dixon (1974) and Hill (1981). Ex- ternal colony form was described using some of the terms of Powell (1980). Species-level systematics are based on both qual- itative and quantitative methods. Quantitative analysis is readi- ly applied to tabulate coral taxonomy because tabulates possess a number of characters that can be quantified by simple mea- surement and are usually good indicators of intraspecific and interspecific variation. Quantitative data should be carefully interpreted, and should not be used to override important qual- itatively-defined features in species recognition and discrimi- nation. Important features that cannot be readily quantified are considered in detail in the descriptive portion of this study.

Techniques used to measure quantitatively analyzed features were, in part, adapted from those defined by Dixon (1974, 1979). To provide data representative of each colony, 20 measurements of each internal character were taken where possible (Dixon, 1974; C. T. Scrutton, personal commun.) on sections made on the upper part of each colony (see Noble and Young, 1984). A colony mean for each character based on such measurements gives a reasonably good representation of the data, as indicated by histograms of distribution for colonies chosen at random. Each feature was measured to the nearest 0.05 mm. Characters that were numerically defined are discussed below.
