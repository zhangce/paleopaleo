In the formations examined, tabulate corals are found in a variety of facies representing a range of shelf environments, but they are absent from certain important ones such as the algal- flat facies of the La Vieille Formation (see Bourque et al., 1986). The heliolitids are more restricted than the other tabulate groups. They are most abundant in facies interpreted as open shelf or as nearshore carbonate banks and reefs, are rare in a silty lime- stone facies that probably represents nearshore shelf environ- ments, and are completely absent from facies interpreted as lagoonal and as shallow-water sand and gravel bars.

The diff'erences in distribution among heliolitid species (Fig- ure 5) are difficult to interpret because the functional advantages of the various morphologies are obscure, and cannot be readily compared numerically except in a general way because expo- sures of the diferent facies are variable. In general, Helialites interstinctus occurs mostly in facies interpreted as open shelf and shallow-mater carbonate shelf, and more rarely in a shallow- water, patch reef facies. Heliolites distinctus is equally common in a carbonate bank facies of the Limestone Point Formation, and in a deeper water facies of the Gascons Formation which may be allochthonous and derived from carbonate banks. He- li olives laxus is restricted to ofBhore facies of the La Vieille and Gascons formations, while the other common large-corallite species, Heliolites sp. 1, occurs only in the carbonate bank facies of the Limestone Point Formation. The single specimen of He- liolites sp. cf. H. megastoma occurs in an assemblage considered to be at the deep-water extreme of the open-shelf facies of the Limestone Point Formation. Stelliporella sp. cf. S. liljevalli is confined to onshore siltstones of the Gascons Formation.
