This glauconitic sand, about two meters thick on the canal at St. Georges, Delaware (Figures 1, 2), represents a condensed succession that contains fragments of ammonites characteristic of the %estern Interior Campanian ammonite zones of Didymoceras stevensoni and Exiteloceras jenneyi. Examples of D. stevensoni and E. jenneyi from the Chesapeake and Delaware Canal were described by Cobban (1970). The geology of the Chesapeake and Delaware Canal was mapped by the U.S. Geological Survey (1967) at a scale of 1:250,000. The Delaware Geological Survey later mapped it at 1:24,000 (Pickett, 1970). Both maps show the Marshalltown Formation and Mount Laurel Sand along the canal from a little east of St. Georges west to the Summit Bridge area.

Owens and Minard (1970) described a section through the Marshalltown and Mount Laurel 4.8 km west of St. Georges, where the Marshalltown is 4.3 m thick and the Mount Laurel is 4.6 m thick. %ell-preserved, calcareous shells of Exogyra ponderosa Roemer, 1849, were noted in the middle of the Mar- shalltown, and phosphatized internal molds of shells were noted in the upper meter or so. Fossils other than borings were not recorded in the Mount Laurel at that locality.
