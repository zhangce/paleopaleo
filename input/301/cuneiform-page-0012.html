The relative abundance of Pectinidae, but absence of the su- perficially similar Propeamussiidae ("mud scallops" ), is evi- dence for a shallow-water depositional environment. The for- mer are most abundant in depths less than 200 m (though they extend down to 6,000 m), whereas propeamussiids are rare in habitats shallower than 60 m, and most common below 150 m (Wailer, 1972). The presence of a large oyster, tentatively iden- tified as a species of I'/emingostrea, is confirmatory evidence for shallow waters.

Foraminifera from the decapod bed at Te Miko also indicate deposition in shallow waters (H. E. G. Morgans, personal com- mun.). In particular, the assemblage contains common agglu- tinated species and some miliolids, but only rare planktonic species. The presence of attached species of Cibicides, including C. notocenica Dorreen, indicates at least moderate bottom cur- rent activity.

The sedimentological evidence, composition of the mega- fauna, and stratigraphic position of the formation as the basal unit of a transgressive sequence in this area are consistent with an inner- or middle-shelf environment. Furthermore, the rarity of planktonic foraminifera suggests an inshore or partly pro- tected environment with only limited oceanic infiuence. The presence of stalked crinoids in the decapod bed may seem anom- alous in this context, as most records of this group of echino- derms are from below 200 m, but some extant isocrinids extend into much shallower waters. For example, Cenocrinus spp. have a depth range of 0-585 m (Rasmussen in Moore and Teichert, 1978, p. T855).
