Characters cited in the diagnosis of the species do serve to distinguish this new species from all others. Among previously described Eocene species, Lyreidus antarcticus Feldmann and Zinsmeister (1984) has a much broader outline, the anterolateral margins diverge at an angle of about 60', the outer margins of the postorbital spines are parallel or slightly divergent poste- riorly, the orbits are more angular in outline, and the orbital fissures are deeper and stronger. Lyreidus waitakiensis Glaessner (1980), from the Tapui Sandstone, Otago (Bortonian, Middle Eocene), is much more rotund and bears two pairs of lateral spines. This species, which is referable to the L. channeri group of species (Feldmann and Zinsmeister, 1984, p. 1054), was re- ported to occur at the coastal section at Pororari (Cslaessner, 1980, p. 178). However, the sole specimen, Canterbury Museum No. zfc 8, is fragmentary and is probably better referred to L. bennetti. Extensive collection of the Pororari section has yielded no specimens with the distinctive morphology of Lyreidus wai- takiensis.

Lyreidus elegans Cslaessner (1960), from the Miocene of Westland, bears the closest resemblance to L. bennetti. How- ever, the frontal margin, which is poorly known on the former species, is divergent posteriorly and the widest point of the carapace occurs at the position of the lateral spines. The overall outline of the carapace of L. elegans is hexagonal whereas that of L. bennetti is octagonal, owing to the curved posterolateral

margin.

This occurrence of a third species of Lyreidus in Eocene rocks of the high southern paleolatitudes reinforces the suggestion (Zinsmeister and Feldmann, 1984) that the genus may have had its origin in this region. Subsequent radiation into lower lati- tudes and the northern hemisphere presumably followed de- velopment of the circumantarctic current system. Furthermore, all Eocene occurrences are in sediments deposited in moderately high-energy habitats thought to be characteristic of inner-shelf settings. This contrasts markedly with the outer-shelf and bathy- al, muddy habitats occupied by extant species (CJriffin, 1970).
