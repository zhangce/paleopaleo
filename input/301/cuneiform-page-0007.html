The origin of the decapod accumulations is discussed in more detail below.

Unit 3 overlies the decapod-bearing beds of unit 2. It consists of about 2 m of somewhat more resistant sandstone that forms overhangs and tends to weather in a boxwork fashion. Body fossils consist of molluscan fragments, which are more numer- ous but also more finely broken than in unit 2. Although scat- tered vertical and horizontal burrow structures are present, they are not as common as in unit 2.

Unit 4 comprises 9 m of alternating sandstone beds of variable resistance to weathering. Each of the more resistant beds of unit 4 weathers as a projecting ledge deeply dissected in a boxwork fashion. The less resistant beds form reentrants. A total of five ledge-recess couplets are present. The more resistant beds pre- dominate in the lower part of the unit, and the softer beds become thicker, up to about 2 m, in the upper part. Fossils are more common and considerably more diverse in unit 4 than in the underlying units, and include bryozoans (delicate branching, ramose, and fenestrate forms), calcareous worm-tubes, brachio- pods, mollusks, regular and irregular echinoids, and the decapod crustacean, I.yreidus bennetti n. sp. Trace fossils are moderately common and include both horizontal, I'/anolites, and vertical, Thallasi noides, burrows. The mode of occurrence of megafossils in unit 4 is also varied. They may occur as beds of finely com- minuted shells; as discrete, complete, articulated individuals (e.g., some brachiopods and echinoids); as discrete fragments (particularly of bryozoans) in concretionary masses; or as clumps of largely monospecific individuals (some brachiopods). The sole decapod crustacean collected from this unit was in a small, isolated concretion about 5 cm in diameter.
