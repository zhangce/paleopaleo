Glaessner noted (1980, p. 178) that the shape and ornamentation of the carapace, as well as the conformation of the fixed finger, served to distinguish R. pororariensis from other members of the genus.

Reexamination of the holotype, as well as study of the extant species of the genus, suggests that the specimen does represent a distinct species but that it is better referred to Laeviranina than to Ranilia. Most species of Ranilia attain their greatest width at, or near, the lateral spines whereas the widest part of the carapace on the sole specimen of L. pororariensis lies at about midlength, well behind the lateral spines. In general out- line, and considering only the species of Ranilia, this species probably most closely resembles R. muricata H. Milne Edwards. However, most species of Laeviranina maintain a constant width along the lateral margins, or become slightly wider posterior to the lateral spines. Perhaps a more significant point of distinction with regard to carapace morphology is that the fronto-orbital region of L. pororariensis lies below the general level of the carapace and is separated from the rest of the carapace by a ridge, or an escarpment, extending between the lateral spines (Figure 6). This kind of structure is absent altogether, or is much less well developed, on species of Ranilia. Finally, the config- uration of the fixed fingers of this specimen does not permit placement within Ranilia. All modern species of Ranilia„as well as R. punctulata, have a propodus that is unique to the genus. The palm is triangular and the vestige of fixed finger is oriented with its long axis parallel to the lower margin of the palm so that it appears as a short, distally directed spine. By contrast, the fixed finger of the specimen of L. pororariensis is well de- veloped and, as in many other raninids including species of Laeviranina, is directed almost at right angles to the long axis of the palm (Figure 5.2). Thus, placement of the species within Ranilia is unwarranted.
