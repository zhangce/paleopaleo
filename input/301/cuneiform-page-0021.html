The fissures tend to be open and obvious and the ridge tends to be reduced or absent in specimens referable to Raninoides, whereas the fissures are small, closed, and subtle and the ridge is better developed on species of Laeviranina. Using these observations as bases for placement until such time as the two genera are restudied in detail, the Westland species is best referred to Laeviranina.

In general outline, L. keyesi also resembles species of Notopus, but the latter has a single orbital fissure and may possess a supraorbital projection, or spine, as well as a postorbital spine.

Laeviranina keyesi divers markedly from L. pororariensis (Glaessner) discussed below, in several ways. The latter is larger, much wider proportionally, and more coarsely punctate. Ap- pendages are not known, with certainty, on L. keyesi, so that there is no possibility of making a comparison of these structures at present. A third species within this genus is also known from New Zealand. Laeviranina perarmata Glaessner was described (1960, p. 15) from the Bortonian rocks of Snowdrift Quarry, Milburn, Otago.
