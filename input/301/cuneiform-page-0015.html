Ingestion by some other crab-feeder (e.g., crabs or possibly ceph- alopods) could also be expected to severely damage the speci- mens. Additionally, the presence of well-preserved, discrete fe- cal pellets within the accumulations argues against the notion that the material has been ingested and expelled. The reiden- tification of the %'aiau material as galatheids rather than pa- gurids makes it easier to account for their occurrence en masse. Galatheids are gregarious and some are pelagic. Thus, they could perish in large numbers as the result of an algal bloom or some other locally catastrophic event. The origin of the Te Miko concretions is more problematical, but a plausible explanation is that the specimens were concentrated by bottom-current ac- tion in vacant crab burrows, or in exhumed spatangoid burrows, even though no obvious association of decapod material with definite burrow structures was noted in the field. The presence of decapod fecal material in many concretions supports the idea that at least some of these structures formed in crab burrows. An alternative possibility, that the eoncretions merely represent sites where shell material accumulated in depressions on the sea floor (e.g., in troughs of ripple structures), seems less likely and does not account for their shape or limited extent.

Conditions, even in shallow burrows, can difter markedly from those prevailing on the sea floor. In particular, if circulation is at all restricted, the microenvironment will be poorly oxy- genated and lead to preservation of skeletal material, especially of crustaceans, that would otherwise disappear. The preserva- tion of turritellids in some nodules suggests that the rate of calcium carbonate solution was su%ciently retarded to allow conversion of aragonite to more stable calcite.
