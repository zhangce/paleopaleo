As decapod crustaceans are potentially very useful for paleo- ecological studies (Maxwell, 1988) and biogeographic analysis (Feldmann, 1986), much eifort has been made in recent years to collect fossil decapods, and a considerable amount of material is now available for study. In this paper we describe a late Eocene decapod faunule that is notable because of its abundant, excel- lently preserved material and exceptionally high decapod species- diversity (eight taxa). We also discuss aspects of the geological occurrence, paleoecology, and biogeographic signi6cance of the faun ule.

The material described here is from the lower part of the Island Sandstone, which is exposed extensively along the coast between Perpendicular Point and Punakaiki, about 38 km north of Greymouth, Westland (Figure 1). The Island Sandstone is widespread in north Westland and typically forms the basal marine transgressive unit in the region; in most places it rests on Brunner Coal Measures (middle upper Eocene), but in the vicinity of Perpendicular Point it directly overlies Meybille Granite (probably middle Paleozoic) (Laird, 1988, p. 11). In the Greymouth district the unit is largely middle Eocene (Bortonian Stage in the local biostratigraphic scheme), but in the Punakaiki district it is significantly younger, mostly upper Eocene (Kaiatan and Runangan Stages) but extending into the lower Oligocene (Whaingaroan Stage). North of Perpendicular Point, toward Woodpecker Bay at the mouth of the Fox River, the Island Sandstone grades laterally into mudstone of the Kaiata For- mation (Laird, 1988, p. 27). South of Punakaiki at Dolomite Point, site of the famed Pancake Rocks, the Island Sandstone is overlain by the Potikohua Limestone (lower Oligocene to lower Miocene); elsewhere, it is typically overlain by the Kaiata Formation.
