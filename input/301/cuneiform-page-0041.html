The smooth chelae are large, relative to the carapace; the hand of the best preserved specimen (Figure 13.2, 13.3) is 10.8 mm long and 7.4 mm high at the distal end, tapering to 5 mm proximally. The fixed finger, which exceeds 7.5 mm in length, is uniformly tapering distally, downturned slightly from the long axis of the base of the hand, and armed with domal denticles such that larger denticles are separated from one another by two or three smaller ones.

Carcinoplax spp. are widely distributed in the western Pacific region in Japan (Sakai, 1976), Australia (Rathbun, 1923), and New Zealand (Bennett, 1964), as well as in the eastern Atlantic (Manning and Holthuis, 1981). Typically, they are inhabitants of the outer shelf and slope.

Fossil Carcinoplax have not been described previously from the Eocene of the Indo-Pacific region.
