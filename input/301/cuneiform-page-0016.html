Paleontological evidence indicates a moderately deep depositional environment in the outer shelf to upper bathyal zone for the formation in the Greymouth district (Srinivasan, 1965, p. 310; Hoskins in Nathan, 1974, p. 16).

Tumidocarcinus tumidus is recorded from a considerable number of localities, mainly in the South Island, and seems to have been bathymetrically tolerant, as it occurs in both shallow- water and moderately deep-water assemblages. Its absence from the decapod bed at Te Miko, however, may imply that this was deposited in significantly shallower waters than the upper limit of its depth range.

Representatives of Laeviranina, Lyreidus, Rhachiosoma, and Leptomithrax have been described from New Zealand previ- ously and all but Leptomithrax have been reported from Eocene rocks. The occurrence of Leptomithrax grani, as well as Car cinoplax temikoensis, and Notomithrax alIani, in the Island Sandstone represents the first record of these genera in Eocene rocks. Thus, these genera may have had their origin in the tem- perate latitudes of the southern hemisphere. Each has congeneric descendants living in the Indo-Pacific region today.
