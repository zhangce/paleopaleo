MODE OF FORMATION OF DECAPOD-BEARING CONCRETIONS

One of the most remarkable features of the decapod bed at Te Miko is that the bulk of the crustacean material occurs in discrete concretions. These concretions contain accumulations of the remains of several individuals and, in some cases, of more than one species. Relatively few specimens occur as single in- dividuals. These aggregations differ markedly from the "ex- traordinary assemblage of crustacean remains" from the %'aiau River, North Canterbury (N32/f9125), described by Glaessner (1960, p. 32). This unlocalized collection, which is probably of Pliocene age, includes what Glaessner described as "hundreds of fragments of legs of decapods, most likely of pagurids, to- gether with a few carapace fragments of Cancer."

Glaessner (1960, p. 32) suggested that the Waiau material represented "the remnants of a meal of a crab-eating animal, possibly a seal."
