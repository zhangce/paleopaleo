The decapod- rich bed lies approximately 5 m above the base of the exposed section. In general, the lithology is moderately cemented, cal- careous, micaceous sandstone, but there is some variation in degree of cementation, in development of "fretted" ~eathering, and in the composition and mode of occurrence of trace and body fossils. Laird (1988, p. 23) noted that the more resistant beds within the Island Sandstone contain up to 60 percent CaCO„ whereas the softer beds average only 20-25 percent. This varia- tion permits subdividing the Te Miko section into five units.

The lowest 2 m of sandstone exposed in the bay is unit 1: a uniformly massive glauconitic sandstone with rare limonitic concretions, and extremely rare, vertical, lined burrows. Body fossils consist primarily of pectinid bivalves, which are typically disarticulated, broken, and randomly oriented. The upper sur- face of this unit is dominated by numerous, poorly defined, back-filled Planoli jes burrows. Some echinoid fragments and terminations of vertical, lined burrow structures are also present.

Unit 2 consists of 3 m of somewhat friable sandstone, which tends to weather more easily than subjacent unit 1. Horizontal and steeply inclined burrow structures, Thallassinoides and Margaritichnus, are more common in this unit than in unit 1, particularly in the lower 1.5 m. Similar burrow structures were observed in the Kaiata Formation near the base of the sequence at %oodpecker Bay, where they are associated with eehinoids and the crabs Tumidocarcinus tumidus and Ahachiosoma gran- ujiferum (Glaessner). Body fossils in this unit are mostly dis- articulated, broken, and randomly arranged, and include bi- valves (predominantly pectinids), irregular echinoids, and polychaete worm tubes. The top of this unit is commonly de- fined by a layer of limonite-cemented sandstone about 1 cm thick.
