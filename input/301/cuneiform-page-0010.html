The only previously published record of megafossils from the Island Sandstone in this area, other than the decapods described by Glaessner {1980), is by Henderson {1975), who identified four species of spatangoid echinoids.

Echinoids, particularly spatangoids, are the most widespread group of megafossils in the Island Sandstone, and are locally common, more so than at most other New Zealand Cenozoic fossil localities. Of the four species recorded by Henderson (1975), Tainuznuwa prima Henderson was described from Gentle Annie Rocks, 0.8 km NE of Perpendicular Point, where it occurs with Schizaster (Paraster) exoletus Hutton. Other echinoid groups are also represented; clypeastreoids (Arachnoides? n. sp.) are common in coarse sandstone at the base of the formation east of Perpendicular Point and regular echinoids occur sporadically in the lower part of the unit.

The only other echinoderms recorded from the Island Sand- stone are rare crinoids in the decapod bed at Te Miko. One incomplete specimen is articulated„has several cirri still at- tached to the stem, and is closely associated with decapod re- mams.

Bivalves are probably the second most abundant group, and include at least three species of pectinids, many specimens of which are either articulated or have only slightly displaced valves. Duptipecten parki (Matwick), originally described as a species of Lentipecten (Beu and Maxwell in Beu, Maxwell, and Brazier, 1989), was described from Bortonian beds at Hampden Beach, North Otago. It replaces its presumed ancestor D. waihaoensis (Suter) in the upper part of the Bortonian in the Waihao River section, South Canterbury, and is recorded from a number of localities of Bortonian, Kaiatan, and probably Runangan age in the South Island, including Westland, and from Chatham Island.
