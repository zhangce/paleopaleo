The Celtic as- semblage of brachiopods has been interpreted to have lived in cool waters of continental margins and intraoceanic sites at mid- to high paleolatitudes, whereas the Toquima/Table Head assem- blage is thought to have lived in warm transequatorial waters (Neuman, 1984). The distinctiveness of these assemblages was supported by statistical tests (Neuman and Harper, 1992; Harper et al., 1996). Nearly one-third (7 of 20) of the brachiopod genera listed from the uppermost biozone of the San Juan Limestone of the Precordillera (Herrera and Benedetto, 1991) occur togeth- er in Celtic assemblages of the North Atlantic borderlands, in- dicating approach of the Precordillera to the Gondwanan margin during Arenig/Llanvirn time (Astini et al., 1995). The smaller assemblage (six genera) in coeval rocks of the Famatina Range includes the distinctive brachiopod, Famatinorthis turneri Levy and Nullo, previously considered to be endemic (Benedetto, 1994); its identification in the archetypal Celtic assemblage of the Shin Brook Formation of northeastern Maine as Famatinor- this cf. F. turneri strengthens the link of the Famatina generic assemblage to the Celtic biogeographic assemblages. Paleogeo- graphic interpretations based on recently obtained paleomagnetic data (Conti et al., 1996) are similar.

Famatinorthis turneri occurs in the Molles and Suri forma- tions (Arenig) of the Famatina Terrane of northwestern Argen- tina. The Famatina Terrane, coextensive with the Famatina Range, is a long, narrow, fault-bounded block whose Ordovician volcanic rocks are interspersed with small amounts of fossilif- erous siliceous sedimentary rocks.
