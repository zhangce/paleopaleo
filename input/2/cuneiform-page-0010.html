Scleractinian corals, for example, do not appear until the Triassic, and carbonate hardgrounds are very rare in the Car- boniferous and Permian (Wilson and Palmer, 1992). Carbonate rocky shore deposits, such as the one described here, will be the most likely producers of future upper Paleozoic Gastrochaen- olites.

Gastrochaenolites was a hard-substrate manifestation of the infaunalization trend noted in association with the rise of duro- phagous predators termed the Mesozoic Marine Revolution (Vermeij, 1977; Palmer, 1982; Wilson and Palmer, 1992; Klee- mann, 1996). The Pennsylvanian Gastrochaenolites, however, predate the first indications of that revolution in the Late Triassic by at least 100 million years. It may be that these borings, i:-+ they were produced by bivalves escaping shell-crushing preda- tors, are part of what Signor and Brett (1984) termed the "mid- Paleozoic precursor" to that revolution, when predators on ben- thic shelled organisms increased dramatically in diversity and numbers. This was also a time when some other endolithic groups that later became common first appeared in the ancient record (Palmer, 1982). Examples are phoronid worms (ichno- genus Talpina), acrothoracican barnacles (ichnogenus Rogerel- la), and the U-shaped worms (ichnogenus Caulostrepsis).
