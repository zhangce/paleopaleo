The G. anauchen n. ichnosp. specimens in this study are all excavated in coarse grainstone cobbles derived from the under- lying Mississippian Pitkin Limestone, and they cut both grains and cement within them. The cobbles are part of a rocky-shore deposit which preserves a diverse assemblage of hard-substrate- dwelling organisms. It in fact is the only unequivocal rocky- shore deposit and fauna known fmm the Pennsylvanian (John- son, 1988, 1992), although Pennsylvanian bored cobbles from a fragmented hardground in Kansas were briefly described by West and Palmer (1983). The cobbles were also bored by ac- rothoracican barnacles, producing small slit-shaped holes, and they bear thc con@non cylindrical worm boring Trypanites. Webb (1993) describes an encrusting tabulate coral from these cobbles, and also (Webb, 1994) notes encrusting bryozoans on the cobble surfaces. This Pennsylvanian cobble-dwelling fauna is lower in diversity than similar fossil faunas in the Ordovician (Wilson, 1985), Devonian (Baird, 1976), and Cretaceous (Wil- son, 1986), but this may be due to a higher level of overturning disturbance (Wilson, 1987).
