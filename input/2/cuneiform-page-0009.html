Many studies of boring on modern carbonate substrates have shown that endolithic bivalves produce Gastrochaenolites (e.g., Warme and McHuron, 1978; Scott, 1988). Sipunculid worms, though, can also produce Gastrochaenolites (Rice, 1969), but so far, these borings are known with certainty only from loosely cemented carbonates such as beachrocks (Wilson, 1997). The shape of G. anauchen n. ichnosp., with its maximum width near the base and its lack of a neck, is most similar to those borings excavated by lithophagids and not gastrochaenids (Fiirsich et al., 1994). Lithophagid bivalves have been described from the Carboniferous (Wilson, 1962, 1965), but they have not yet been associated with borings. We cannot be sure, then, that the Pennsylvanian borings described here were formed by en- dolithic bivalves, but we can say that the borings are indistin- guishable from those Gastrochaenolites, which were definitely bivalve-excavated.

ORIGIN OF BIVALVE BORING

The first bivalve boring known in the fossil record is Petrox- esres Wilson and Palmer, 1988, a medium-sized slit-shaped bor- ing in the Upper Ordovician of the upper midwestern USA. It was apparently produced by the modiomorphacean bivalve Cor- allidomus, which was a facultative rather than an obligate borer (Pojeta and Palmer, 1976). Petroxestes is known only from the Late Ordovician, and is the only bivalve boring described in the Paleozoic except for the possibly bivalve-produced Gastro- chaenolites of this study. The Pennsylvanian Gastrochaenolites suggest that some Paleozoic lithophagid bivalves were actual borers rather than nestlers as pteviously proposed (Kelly and Bromley, 1984), and that the obligate bivalve macroboring habit was established as early as the Carboniferous.
