VIII. COMPARISONS WITH OTHER AREAS

At the end of Lower Carboniferous times in the north of England, there were two massifs separated by a subsiding basin. In the north was the Lake District-Pennine massif with its thin veneer of Carboniferous sedi- ments, and to the south was the Derbyshire massif, whilst the basin (the Central Province) extended across the central Pennine area.

The Millstone Grit facies accumulated largely in the Central Basin, which itself can be divided into two parts. The northern part has the thickest and most complete development of the series in Great Britain and is characterized by a great thickness of sediments in the Pendleian and Arnsbergian stages. The region of maximum thickness extends from Rombalds Moor westwards to Blackburn and is parallel to the edge of the northern massif. The Lancaster Fells lie just to the north of this region. The southern part of the basin, in contrast, has only thin deposits referable to the Lower Namurian; the major development of grits being in the upper part of the sequence.
