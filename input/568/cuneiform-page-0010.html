(j) The Crossdale Grit

This grit is thickest in its most southerly and westerly outcrops and thins towards the north-east, where it contains a thin coal seam. In the west, the coarse and pebbly false-bedded sandstone which forms Nottage Crag {565.654) (Slinger 1936) can almost certainly be assigned to this horizon. South of Nottage Crag, on Caton Moor, only the lowest beds are present, and these have passed largely into flags {see below), Farther east the grit is medium in grain and, with its marine roof, is exposed in the Summersgill, White Moss district (Fig. 7; 64.64). In Crossdale it thins to 16 feet, is rather inicaceous and contains a seven-inch coal seam with a fireclay seat-earth.

Slinger's suggested sequence (1936) placed the shales of Claughton Moor (576.649) and the succeeding flags (termed Moorcock Flags) above the Nottage Crag (Crossdale) Grit. The base of the Nottage Crag Grit has, however, been mapped round Caton Moor and has been shown to be not below, but at the same horizon as, the base of the Moorcock Flags. The Hawkshead Gnt (635.603), which Slinger equated with the Moorcock Flags, is considered herein to be somewhat higher and equivalent to the lower part of the Bentham Grit Group.

Marine shales between 40 and 50 feet thick rest on the Crossdale Grit, For the most part they are dark, fine-grained, soft and irregularly bedded, and contain rare lamellibranchs of the Posidoni ella type.
