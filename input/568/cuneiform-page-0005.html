The Lower Brennand Grit is about 200 feet thick, and is usually fels- pathic, extremely pebbly, and has a tendency towards false-bedding. It maintains essentially the same thickness from upper Wyresdale to upper YVhitendale. The sandy shale which follows is up to 30 feet thick, and, although not often exposed, is easily mapped by feature.

The Upper Brennand. Grit varies from a pebbly to a medium-grained felspathic sandstone, and like the lower member is false-bedded in places. It maintains a fairly uniform thickness of just over 100 feet, although in Whitendale there is a tendency for this to increase and for a shale parting to develop in the middle. Near Caton in the north-west there are exposures of a thick pebbly felspathic sandstone referable to the Bren- nand Grits. It is not possible here to distinguish upper and lower members.

In 1943 Hudson 4 Cotton suggested the division of this stage into the three zones of Eumorphoceras bisuicatum (EE„), Cravenoceratoides nitidus (Ktb), and Xueuloeeras nuculum, (E„). In the Lancaster Eells strata of Arnsbergian age are very important and crop out over fully three-quarters of the total area. The first two of the above-mentioned zones are well represented, but the marine band containing X. nueulum does not appear to be present.
