A remnant of a still higher unit is preserved below regionally truncating Pennsylvanian unconformities (Figure 1) only in a rather limited area in north-central Arkansas, in New- ton, Searcy, Stone, and Van Buren Counties. The name Imo Formation was proposed for these youngest Chesterian shales by Gordon (1964), who abandoned the name in a footnote in the same paper because Glick et al. (1964) included these Mis- sissippian shales with unconformably overlying Pennsylvanian shales in a newly named Cane Hill Formation. The assumption by Glick et al. was that such a shale-on-shale sequence could not be separated into mappable units based on lithologic criteria. However, Hawkins (1983) provided stratigraphic evidence in- dicating that these two shales can be consistently differentiated on, lithologic grounds. Thus, the name Imo should be reinstated under' Article 20, part (e) of the North American Stratigraphic Code (1983). This contention is also supported by the fact that a large majority of papers published in the intervening decades have used the term Imo and not Cane Hill for the uppermost Mississippian shales in question (e.g., Saunders, 1973; Brenckle, 1977; Manger, 1977; %'ebb, 1984; Mapes and Rexroad, 1986; and Hoare et al., 1989).

The Imo Formation consists of as much as 104 m of dark- gray to black, fissile, clay shale that contains numerous, large, bright-red, orange, or brown concretions. The shales are gen- erally not silty. In contrast, the overlying Cane Hill shales are silty and platy, and contain scattered orange to dull red con- cretions. There are also thin interbeds of siltstone and scattered thick to massive sandstone beds throughout (Hawkins, 1983, table 1). The lower 36 m of the Imo Formation contains thin limestones, sandstones, and conglomerates. Some of these lime- stones and adjacent shales are fossiliferous and have produced the fauna described herein, as well as the previously mentioned ammonoid, conodont, and foraminiferal faunas.

At several Imo localities the Cane Hill Formation has been removed by post-Cane Hill erosion related to unconformities at the base of the Mitts Spring or the Middle Bloyd Sandstone (Figure 1). In these cases the Mississippian-Pennsylvanian boundary is represented by a sandstone on shale contact (Haw- kins, 1983).
