PALEOENVIRON MENT

Imo corals occur in thin limestones and associated shales in the lower part of the formation. Corals occurring in Imo lime- stones are invariably randomly oriented and are in many cases abraded. Those occurring in shale are typically disoriented, but unabraded, having intact, although slightly crushed, calices. They rarely occur in growth position. Heavily abraded specimens rarely occur in shale with unabraded specimens. The branching tabulate coral, Tectamichelinia, occurs mostly in shale and is invariably broken into segments. Imo limestone beds are typ- ically less than 1 m thick and consist of very coarse crinoidal grainstones and packstones that contain varying amounts of terrigenous clay. They contain a diverse, normal marine fauna consisting of crinoids, bryozoans, brachiopods, bivalves, corals, and nautiloids. The fauna of the associated shale is dominated by infaunal bivalves, nautiloids, goniatites, and corals, but scat- tered brachiopods and crinoids occur. Hawkins {1983) stated that higher in the formation, where corals have so far not been collected, the fauna becomes more characteristic of a brackish environment and sandstones contain terrestrial plant fragments. The environment of deposition is thus interpreted as a relatively shallow-water, low-energy offshore area undergoing slow, con- tinuous deposition of terrigenous clay with periodic higher en- ergy incursions of carbonate sediments.
