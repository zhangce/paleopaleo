The Imo coral fauna is very distinct from that of the con- formably underlying Pitkin Formation (Webb, 1987). This ap- pears to be due to difterent environments of deposition. Whereas the Imo corals occur in shale, the majority of Pitkin corals occur in shallow-water, high-energy oolitic and bioclastic grainstones associated with small thrombolite bioherms. Amplexizaphrentis and Barytichisma, the only two genera that are common to both formations, generally occur in black shales and/or shaly lime- stones in the Pitkin Formation. Lophophyllidium has long been known to occur preferentially in shales (Jeffords, 1947, 1948) and coral assemblages containing I.ophophyllidium, Brady- phyllum, and Amplexizaphrentis were considered to represent "deep water" depositional environments by Cocke (1989). San- do (1980) considered AmplexizapItrentis as part of his shallow- water biofacies but he reported deep-water occurrences as well. Neither Pitkin nor Imo corals represent deep-water assemblages. The Imo Formation represents the uppermost, nearshore part of a regional regressive phase aftecting the entire upper Ches- terian in the Ozark region (Hawkins, 1983). This phase com- menced with the deep-water Fayetteville Shale, progressed through the shallow-water Pitkin Formation, and ended with the hiatus at the top of the Imo Formation (Figure 2). Imo shales presumably were introduced onto the Pitkin shelf owing to the increasing proximity of a terrigenous source area associated with a regressing shoreline. Thus, biostratigraphic changes aside, the transition from Pitkin to Imo coral assemblages represents the replacement of a dominantly carbonate assemblage by a muddy bottom assemblage, without necessitating a major change in water depth.
