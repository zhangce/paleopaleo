Lophophylhdium eastoni, described by Moore and JefIords (1945) from rocks that they presumed to be of Morrowan or Atokan age from northern Arkansas, exhibits large dimensions, thick wall, large columella, and prominent stereoplasmic thick- ening very similar to L. imoense, but it is described as lacking rhopaloid septa, a prominent feature of L. imoense. Lopho- phyllidium eastoni is also possibly more conical in shape, and does not appear to contain septal lamellae in its columella. The species was described from just two specimens that were re- posited in the Walker Museum. They do not appear in the catalog of the Field Museum of Natural History (Windle et al., 1973), where Walker Museum specimens are currently repos- ited, and are presumed lost. The type specimens were collected by W. H. Easton from a "shale of Morrowan or Bendian [Ato- kan] age," 1.4 km east of Leslie, Arkansas (Moore and JeR'ords, 1945, p. 110). This locality was visited, but only Pitkin lime- stone could be discerned, overlain by covered slopes. The Imo Formation overlies the Pitkin Limestone in the vicinity so it is possible that the original L. eastoni specimens came from the Imo Formation and not from higher Pennsylvanian strata. The two species appear to be closely related in any case.
